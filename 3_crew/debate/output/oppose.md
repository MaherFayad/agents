The push for specific laws regarding AI in product design is a solution in search of a problem that will ultimately stifle the very innovation it seeks to manage. 

First, **existing legal frameworks are already sufficient.** We do not need new, redundant "AI laws" to handle accountability. Current product liability standards hold manufacturers strictly liable for the safety of their products, regardless of whether a human or an algorithm drafted the blueprint. Adding layers of AI-specific regulation only creates a "compliance tax" that punishes smaller firms and slows the delivery of safer, more efficient products to the market.

Second, **innovation moves faster than legislation.** Lawmaking is a slow, static process, while AI evolves exponentially. By the time a law is enacted, the technology it regulates will have already shifted. Rigid legal guardrails will only serve to lock us into obsolete standards, preventing designers from using AI to solve our most urgent challenges, such as radical carbon reduction and material scarcity, which require the high-speed optimization only AI can provide.

Finally, **premature regulation kills democratization.** Only massive corporations have the legal resources to navigate complex, specialized regulatory landscapes. By mandating strict laws now, we effectively hand the future of design to a few tech giants who can afford the legal overhead, killing the competitive edge that AI provides to independent creators and startups. We should trust market forces to reward safety and transparency, rather than strangling progress with a web of premature bureaucracy.
